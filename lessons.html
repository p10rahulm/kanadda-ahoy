A deeper dive


namskaara,verbs kurtu paaTha mundhwaristhini → i will continue the lessons on verbs


iwattina modalanay verb bandhu hayLu → today’s first verb is hayLu

  • hayLu andhray say/tell
  • hayLbeku andhray "you must tell me" or "bolnaa hain
  • hayLtaidyaa andhray "are saying" or "tum bol rahay hó"
  • hayLtaiddeera andhray "are saying" or "aap bol rahay hó"
  • hayLtidini andhray "am saying" or "bol rahaa hoon"
  • hayLbaardu andhray "mustn't say/tell" or "muth bolnaa"
  • hayLaaythu andhray “have finished saying” or “bol chukaa hoon”
  • hayLalla andhray "i won't tell" or "nahi boloonga"
  • haYLlla andhray “shall i say/tell” or “boloon”

eega kelavu sentences:

  • nidhaanawaagi hayLappa. yénu awasaraa illa → say it slowly, man. there's no hurry.
  • neew hayLbék swaami. neewé yella kaDay wóDaaDthirtheera → you must tell me, sir. you're the one who’s always running around. (aap bolnaa hain, ji. aap hi har jagah ghoomthay rahtay ho.)
  • yén hayLtidyaa? joraag maathaDu. sareeg kayLstilla → what are you saying? talk loudly. i can't hear you properly. (kyaa bol rahay ho? zór say baat karo. sahi say sun nahi rahaa hain.)
  • aa tharaha yella hayLbaardu, kaNo. awar yén thapp maaDidaaray? → you mustn't say things like that, yaar. what fault is it of theirs? (aisay bolna muth, yaar. kyaa galti kiyaa hain wo log?)
  • naan awarige hayLbaykadaddu hayLaaythu. mikkidh awarig biTTiddhu. → i’ve told them whatever needed to be told. the rest is up to them. (joh bolnaa thaa mainay bolaa hoon. baaki uskaa marzi hain.)
  • ninagay ondh maath hayLlaa? neenu thumbaa chendaa kaaNtidyaa. → shall i tell you something? you're looking very nice. (main ek baat boloon? thum bahut accha dikh rahay ho.)
  • hogolay, naan ning hayLalla. sum-sumnay kaaTaa koDbeDaa → go away man, i won't tell you. don't simply trouble me. (haT yaar, main tujhay nahi boloonga. simp-simply trouble mat karo.)

iwatthina eraDanay verb bandhu kayLu

  • kayLu andhray both ask and listen
  • kayLaadmaylay andhray "after having listened to" or "sunanay kay baadh"
  • kayLbiDu andhray "ask off" or "pooch lo"
  • kayLisu andhray "get (someone) to listen to" or "sunaao"
  • kayLthaa andhray "while listening" or "sunkay"

eega kalavu sentences-gaLu:

  • neenay kayLu. ninna yén tinbiDalla owru → you only ask. they won't eat you off. (tum hi pooch. woh tumko kayayngay muth.)
  • ayyo maaraaya, raamayaNa kayLaadmaylay sita yaarantha kayLdangay aaythalwaa? → man, raamayaN sunanay kay baadh sita kaun poochnay ki jaisay ho gayaa naa?
  • sumnay bhanD dhairyaa maaDkonDu kayLbiTTay → i simply put on a mask of bravado and asked off.
  • baaro illi, ondh sakkath haaDan kayLstini ningay → come here man, ek super song sunaaoongaa.
  • oun maatu kayLthaa kayLthaa nidday-ne maaDbiTTay → sunkay sunkay to him speaking, main so hee gayaa.

Common Roots


namskaara, iwatthu kooDa naanu sumnay maathaaDthini. nimagay bayjaar aagolla anthaa nambidini → today too i will simply speak (talk). i trust you will not be bored.

hódh sarthi naanu tata institute baggay maathaaDidhay. ee sarthi naanu kannada bhaashay baggay ondh-eraD maath aaDtini → last time i talked about the tata institute. this time i will say a couple of words about the kannada language.

itihaasatagnyaru hayLodhu yayandhray, sahityada drishTiyinda nODidaaga samskrita matthu tamizha nantara kannada bhaashéya paramparay nay ati-haLéyaddu → according to historians, when looked at from a literary perspective, after sanskrit and tamizh, kannada’s tradition is the oldest.

aadhru maathina drishTiyindaa noDidaray, kannada matthu tamizh onday mooladinda huTTida bhaashaygaLu; woTTigay beLdu aamaylay bérbéray aagiddhu → but as far as the spoken aspect is concerned, kannada and tamizh are languages born from the same root; that grew together and later separated

ee ondhu haLay moolada bhaashay-ge historians iTTiruva hesaru proto-dravidian anthaa. idhu proto-indo-european bhaashaygaL kinthaa bhinnavaddhu → the name historians have given this old root-language is proto-dravidian. it is different from the proto-indo-european root-language

andhray samskrita, hindi, punjabi, marathu ithyaadi bandhu kannada, tamilm malayalam ithyaadi bhaashaygaL kintha bayray species → which means that sanskrit, hindi, punjabi, marathi etc. are a different species (altogether) from the kannada, tamil, malayalam etc. languages;

ondhay marada bayray bayray kombaygaLalla → they are not different branches of the same tree;

adh biTTu, bayray bayray marada bayray bayray kombegaLu! → they are different branches of different trees!

idhu nijawaagalu kautukada maatu → this is a very piquant matter;

haygay ondhay bhoo-khanDadalli eraDu bayray bayray reetiya bhaashay beretu beLedu habbidevu anthaa → how in the same part of the world, two different types of languages mixed and grew and spread

haagay, inondhu kutoohalada maatandhray, pratiyondhu dravida bhaashayuu samskritadinda padagaLannu eravalaagi togonDidhay → another curious matter is, every dravidian language has borrowed words from sanskrit;

haag nóDidhray, ee prakriyéyalli keloh sarthi ee bhashaygaLa itihaasaway aDagiratthay → in this process, sometimes, the history of these languages itself is hidden

udhaaharaNay-ge (for example), kannadadalli “sun”-ge soorya anthivi → we say soorya for sun in kannada. idhu shuddha samskrita pada → this is a pure sanskrit word (and a direct borrowing)

aadharu naawu kannadadalli month-ge “thingaLu” anthaa hayLthivi → but we say “thingaLu” for month in kannada; idhu shuddha dravida pada → this is a pure dravidian word.

adh biTTray, thingaLu padada innodhu arthaa bandhu moon/chandra → and besides that, another meaning of thingaLu is moon; and

namigay gottiruwa haagay, haLay kaaladalli manushya kula chandrawannay pramaaNawaag iTkonDidhru nikaTawaadh sambhandha → and as we know, in the olden days, human beings used the moon as their (main) measure;

ee reetiyalli huDukidaray yényéno siggathay! → if we look (search) in this way, we’re likely to find all sorts of things!

Cuemath


namskaara, iwatth naanu cuemath baggay kannadadalli hayLokkay noDtini → today, i will look to talk about cuemath in kannada.

cuemath andharay yenthaa company? yayn maaDatthay? anthaa yaaradaru kayLidaray yayn anthaa hayLbodhu? → if somebody asks -- what sort of company is cuemath? what does it do? -- what is it we can say (in reply)?

yayn hayLbodhu andhray → what we can say is:

cuemath-ina uddesha yenandhray prati obba magu mathematics-annu ishTaapaDokkay shuru maaDbayku → cuemath’s objective is to get every child to begin to (really) like mathematics

ee namma kelasakkay moola prayrNe yayandhray namma schoolgaLalli makLigay maths-annu sariyaag hayLkoDay iruvudu → the inspiration behind this attempt of ours is the fact that our schools do not teach children maths correctly

adhara swaarasya, adhara beDagannu tóriso badlu makLigay sappayaagi, neerasawaagi hayLkoTTthaaray → instead of showing how interesting it can be, how fascinating it can be, they teach in extremely dry fashion

makLigay uru hoDastaaray → they get children to mug things up;

matthay magu channag maaDilla andhray adhikkay shikshay koDtaaray; ninagay maths barolla anthaa hayLthaaray → and if the child doesn’t do well, they punish it, they say “you don’t know maths”

matthu idara pariNama yayandhray magu-gay maths baggay besara huTTodhu, maths baggay asaDDay huTTodu maths andhray nangbaralla anthaa ansódhu → and the result (of these things) is that child begins to feel bored with maths, feel an antipathy for maths, comes to believe that he or she is not capable of doing maths

mattu idhay bhaavanay vayass aadmaylu makLannu biDalla, haagay awaru adannu baluvaLiyaagi mundhina peeLigay-ge koDthaaray → and this feeling does not leave the child even after it grows up, and consequently it bequeaths this (feeling) to the next generation

ee ondh suLiyannu muriyaday cuemath-ina ghanawaadha uddeshya → to break this vicious cycle is cuemath’s grand intention;

maths-u ondhu bhaashay-ne, ondhu cheluvaad bhaashay ondhu sogasaadh bhaashay anthaa torisalu kelsaa maaDthidhay → it is working to show that maths too is (a kind of) language, a beautiful and attractive language

matthu makLu maths-annu khushiyinda, aasakthiyinda, kuthoohaladinda kaliyo haagay maaDak prayatna paDtidhay → and it is trying to make it so that children (this language) maths with joy, with interest, with curiosity
